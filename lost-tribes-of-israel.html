The Ten Lost Tribes of Israel

The ten lost tribes of Israel were exiled by Assyria in 722 BCE and never recorded returning. Where did they go? The theories, the routes, and what we can and can’t know.

In 722 BCE, ten of Israel’s twelve tribes were carried into exile and vanished from the record. Their fate is one of history’s greatest riddles.

The exile of 722 BCE

After the death of King Solomon, the united kingdom of Israel split in two. The southern kingdom of Judah, centred on Jerusalem, was home to the tribes of Judah and Benjamin. The larger northern kingdom kept the name Israel and held the other ten tribes — Reuben, Simeon, Issachar, Zebulun, Dan, Naphtali, Gad, Asher, and the two Joseph tribes of Ephraim and Manasseh.

In the eighth century BCE, the Assyrian Empire was the superpower of the ancient Near East, and its policy toward conquered peoples was deportation. Over a series of campaigns culminating in the fall of the northern capital Samaria around 722 BCE, the Assyrian kings Shalmaneser V and Sargon II broke the northern kingdom and carried much of its population away. This is not disputed folklore; it is recorded in the Hebrew Bible (2 Kings 17) and, independently, in Assyrian royal inscriptions. Sargon II himself boasted of deporting 27,290 people from Samaria — a number describing the elite and a portion of the population, not every last Israelite.

The deportees were resettled, according to the biblical account, ‘in Halah and in Habor by the river of Gozan, and in the cities of the Medes’ — regions corresponding to upper Mesopotamia and north-western Iran. Foreign populations were moved in to replace them. From that point, the northern ten tribes disappear from the historical record as distinct, organised tribes.

Why they were ‘lost’

‘Lost’ is a slightly misleading word, and it is worth being precise about what actually happened, because the mystery lives in the gap between two facts.

The first fact: the northern tribes were deported and never recorded returning as tribes. When the southern kingdom of Judah was later exiled to Babylon in 586 BCE, a substantial community returned decades afterward, rebuilt Jerusalem, and preserved its identity — and it is from Judah that the word ‘Jew’ descends. The ten northern tribes have no such documented homecoming. No chronicle records them re-emerging under their tribal banners.

The second fact: people do not simply evaporate. The likeliest historical outcome is prosaic — that most of the deported northern Israelites were gradually absorbed into the populations around them, intermarrying and assimilating over generations until their distinct identity dissolved. Some almost certainly remained in the land and merged with the southern kingdom or with the population that became the Samaritans, whose small community survives to this day.

So they were ‘lost’ in the sense that a distinct, trackable Israelite identity was lost — not necessarily that ten whole nations marched off intact to a secret destination. That distinction is exactly where careful history and speculative theory part ways.

The migration theories

The absence of a recorded return created a vacuum, and for more than two thousand years people have tried to fill it. The theories range from the historically grounded to the frankly imaginative.

  • Absorption and assimilation — the mainstream historical view. The deportees were dispersed across the Assyrian Empire and gradually merged into surrounding peoples, leaving descendants who no longer identified as Israelite. Undramatic, but well supported by how ancient deportations generally worked.
  • Northward and westward migration. A family of theories — British-Israelism among them — proposes that groups of exiles migrated over centuries through the Caucasus and across Europe, and that identifiable descendants survive among European peoples. Advocates point to peoples such as the Scythians and Cimmerians as intermediaries. Mainstream scholars regard these links as unproven and the linguistic and archaeological evidence as not supporting them.
  • Eastern dispersion. Other traditions trace exiles eastward into Persia, Central Asia, Afghanistan, and India, connecting them to groups that later preserved Israelite customs or claims.
  • The legendary Sambatyon. A body of Jewish legend places the tribes beyond a mythical river, the Sambatyon, that rages six days a week and rests on the Sabbath — a poetic way of saying they lie beyond reach, in a realm more of hope than of geography.

It is worth holding these apart in your mind. The first is history; the rest are hypotheses of varying strength, and the honest reader keeps track of which is which.

Claimed descendants around the world

Across the globe, a striking number of communities have claimed — or been claimed by others — to descend from the lost tribes. Some of these traditions are ancient and internally held; others were assigned from the outside. A few have been recognised in limited ways by Jewish authorities; most have not.

  • The Bene Israel and Bnei Menashe of India, the latter in the north-east of the country, who trace themselves to the tribe of Manasseh. Some Bnei Menashe have undergone formal conversion and emigrated to Israel.
  • The Beta Israel of Ethiopia, historically linked in tradition to the tribe of Dan, most of whom now live in Israel.
  • The Pashtuns of Afghanistan and Pakistan, among whom a persistent tradition of Israelite descent survives, alongside customs that some observers connect to Israelite practice.
  • Western and northern European peoples, under British-Israelism and related movements — the claim that Britain, and by extension nations of British descent, carry the line of Ephraim and Manasseh.
  • Various communities in Africa, Central Asia, and the Americas who maintain Israelite-origin traditions of differing antiquity and evidence.

The pattern itself is telling. The idea of the lost tribes has enormous cultural gravity, and communities on every continent have found meaning in claiming a share of it. That does not make the claims true or false by itself — each must be weighed on its own evidence — but it explains why the tribes turn up, so to speak, nearly everywhere at once.

What the evidence supports

So where does an honest reckoning leave us? It helps to separate what is documented, what is plausible, and what is unknowable.

Documented: the northern kingdom of Israel existed, the Assyrians deported a large part of its population around 722 BCE, and those deportees are never recorded returning as distinct tribes. This is solid ground, attested by both biblical and Assyrian sources.

Plausible but unproven: that identifiable descendants survive in specific modern communities. Some communities have oral traditions and, in a few cases, suggestive cultural or genetic signals. But genetics has a hard limit here that no amount of enthusiasm can overcome: DNA can reveal broad ancestry and Middle-Eastern-linked markers, yet it cannot prove descent from a specific lost tribe, because no verified reference genome for the tribes of Israel exists to compare against. A result can be a genuine research lead. It can never be a certificate.

Most likely, and least dramatic: that the bulk of the ten tribes were absorbed into the surrounding peoples of the ancient Near East and passed their ancestry quietly down countless mixed lines — meaning fragments of Israelite descent may be widely scattered through many populations without any single people being ‘the’ lost tribes.

None of this diminishes the fascination of the question. The tribes really were lost, the search for them really has spanned millennia, and your own place in that long story is worth investigating — carefully, with the evidence in front of you and the honest gaps left open.
