Am I Israel? a question millions have started to ask

Are you a descendant of the lost tribes of Israel?

For 2,700 years, ten tribes of Israel have been called “lost.” A long tradition holds that their descendants never vanished — they migrated west, into Britain, Europe and beyond. If that story runs through your family, you may be one of them.

The tribes that walked out of history

In 722 BCE the Assyrian empire conquered the northern kingdom of Israel and carried ten of its twelve tribes into exile. They were never recorded returning. Judah — today’s Jewish people — remained. The other ten became the great riddle of history: the Ten Lost Tribes.

Where did they go? One answer, argued for centuries and known as British-Israelism, is that they moved north and west along the great migration routes — through the Caucasus and Europe — and that their blood runs today in the peoples of Britain, Ireland, and the lands they settled. It is a heritage theory, disputed by mainstream scholars and cherished by those who feel it in their own family story. This project exists for people who want to explore it honestly.

Signs the question might be yours

None of these prove anything on their own. Together, they are why so many people start asking.

A pull toward Israel

An unexplained sense of kinship with the Jewish people and the land of Israel, stronger than your upbringing accounts for.

Family half-memories

Stories of “Hebrew” roots, Sabbath-like customs, or names that were never quite explained.

Old-world migration

Ancestry from the British Isles, north-western Europe, or the places the tribes were said to pass through.

The names in the land

Curiosity about the Hebrew echoes some hear in old European place-names, words, and heraldry.
