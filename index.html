The Legend — Tea Tephi, Daughter of Zedekiah

In 586 BCE, Jerusalem fell to Babylon. King Zedekiah, last king of the House of David, watched his sons killed before his own eyes were put out. But the annals of Ireland tell a stranger coda to that catastrophe: that the prophet Jeremiah did not flee to Egypt alone. Tradition holds he carried with him one of Zedekiah’s surviving daughters — remembered in Irish lore as Tea Tephi — along with a rough stone Jacob himself was said to have rested his head on at Bethel centuries before.

The story continues west: through Egypt, through Iberia, and finally to Ireland, where Tea Tephi is said to have married the High King and become the ancestress of a royal line. The stone she carried — the Lia Fáil, “Stone of Destiny” — became the seat on which Irish, then Scottish, then English monarchs were crowned. Edward I hauled it to Westminster in 1296. It sits under the coronation chair to this day, beneath every British monarch crowned since — including, by tradition, the present one.

If the story is true, the Davidic throne promised in scripture never actually ended in 586 BCE. It simply moved.

Echoes in the Language — Three Welsh Phrases, Startlingly Close to Hebrew

Welsh–Hebrew word parallels have been documented since at least 1675, when the Welsh clergyman Charles Edwards first catalogued them in Hanes y Ffydd. Mainstream linguists classify Welsh (Indo-European) and Hebrew (Afro-Asiatic) as unrelated language families — but researchers in the British-Israel tradition point to matches like these as too close to dismiss as coincidence.

BAGAD“A troop cometh” (Genesis 30:11)בגדHebrew Bagad — identical in both sound and spelling.
ANUDON“Without God”אין אדוןHebrew Ain Adon, “there is no Lord” — near-identical construction and sound.
AN ANNOS“None did compel” (Esther 1:8)אין אונסHebrew Ain Ones, “there is no compulsion” — matching both meaning and sound.

These parallels are part of the theory this site explores. Mainstream scholarship does not accept a Welsh–Hebrew link; we present them as intriguing claims to weigh, not settled fact.

The tribes that walked out of history

In 722 BCE the Assyrian empire conquered the northern kingdom of Israel and carried ten of its twelve tribes into exile. They were never recorded returning. Judah — today’s Jewish people — remained. The other ten became the great riddle of history: the Ten Lost Tribes.

Where did they go? One answer, argued for centuries and known as British-Israelism, is that they moved north and west along the great migration routes — through the Caucasus and Europe — and that their blood runs today in the peoples of Britain, Ireland, and the lands they settled. It is a heritage theory, disputed by mainstream scholars and cherished by those who feel it in their own family story. This project exists for people who want to explore it honestly.
